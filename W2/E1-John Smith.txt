Question 1: Correlation vs. Causation
The map alone only showed that many deaths were concentrated near the Broad Street pump, which is correlation. Snow moved toward causation by collecting more detailed exposure data. He went house to house, recorded the exact location of deaths, and asked where people got their drinking water from. He also tested his hypothesis against exceptions, which made his reasoning much stronger. Nearby groups such as brewery workers and people in the workhouse had low death rates because they used other water sources, while a woman living far away still died because she regularly drank water from Broad Street. In effect, Snow used a natural experiment: people in similar areas had different outcomes depending on whether they consumed water from that pump. That moved his argument from “deaths happen near the pump” to “the pump water is the cause.”
Question 2: The Danger of Missing Data
If Snow had only collected hospital data, his dataset would have been much narrower and more misleading. He likely would have missed many people who died at home, missed details about which water source they used, and missed unusual cases that helped test his theory. Hospital records alone might still have shown that Soho had many cholera deaths, but that would have reinforced the existing miasma explanation, since Soho was both crowded and foul-smelling. By walking the streets and interviewing households, Snow added the crucial variable of water source and identified the anomalies that mattered most. Without that fuller dataset, he might have concluded only that Soho was a dangerous area rather than discovering the specific mechanism behind the outbreak.
Question 3: The “Miasma” of Today
A modern example is the assumption that higher user engagement always means a better product. On the surface, that seems reasonable: if people spend more time in an app, the product must be valuable. But careful data analysis might show the opposite. Users may stay longer because the interface is confusing, the workflow is inefficient, or the product is designed to keep them scrolling rather than helping them succeed. In that case, time spent is only the visible pattern, not the true mechanism. The variables that matter more might be task completion rate, user-reported satisfaction, retention for the right reasons, or how quickly users achieve what they came to do. Like miasma theory, the accepted explanation appears to fit the facts, but deeper analysis may show that the real cause is something else.
